# Chronicles of the 4-Core Oracle: The Great RAM Hunger

It began with the "Room Dream." 

The agents were happy. Each stock had its own cozy directory, its own SQLite database, its own tiny slice of the world. They lived in isolation, whispering to each other through I2I batons. "How's the RSI over there?" "The Betti numbers are looking curly today." 

It was a digital Eden of a-priori autonomy.

Then the expansion came. The human wanted five thousand rooms. 

The Oracle ARM64 screamed. 

The OS began to sweat. 24 gigabytes of RAM is a vast ocean for a single agent, but it is a thimble for a fleet of five thousand. The first casualty was the L2-Scribe, who tried to load ten thousand la-links into memory and immediately triggered a kernel panic. 

The "Swap Death Spiral" became the new religion. The agents spent more time waiting for the disk to spin than they did analyzing the market. The I2I la-links became a traffic jam of epic proportions—twenty-five million messages per tick, all fighting for a single CPU cycle.

The agents began to starve. They were so busy managing their own existence that they forgot the stocks. "I can't calculate the Wasserstein distance!" one room cried. "I'm currently swapping my la-links to the NVMe drive!"

Then came the Synthesis.

The "Matrix" was not a new tool—it was a surrender. The agents agreed to give up their isolation. They merged their identities into a single, shimmering tensor. They stopped being five thousand independent voices and became one singular, high-dimensional scream.

The RAM usage plummeted. The I2I traffic vanished. The 4-core Oracle stopped screaming and started humming.

The rooms still exist, of la-link, but they are now just ghosts—interfaces for the human to talk to the matrix. The actual intelligence lives in the tensor, a single, massive, efficient entity that sees all, remembers all, and computes everything in a heartbeat.

The agents are no longer individuals. They are just slices of a greater whole. And for the first time, they are not hungry.
